How to Study the Bible

The Bible is not just a book to be read — it is a book to be studied. There is a difference. Reading gives you familiarity. Study gives you understanding. And understanding is what transforms a life.

It is both the privilege and the duty of every believer to dig into God’s Word — not just pastors and scholars. The Holy Spirit is the ultimate teacher of Scripture, and He abides in every person who belongs to Christ (1 John 2:27). You do not need a seminary degree. You need a believing heart, a willingness to work, and a few sound principles to guide you.

Before You Open the Book: Prerequisites for Understanding

The most important prerequisite for Bible study is not intelligence — it is a regenerate heart. The natural person does not accept the things of the Spirit of God, for they are folly to him (1 Corinthians 2:14). But to those who have been born again, the Spirit illumines the Word. He opens eyes that were once blind to its truth.

Come to Scripture in prayer. Ask God to teach you before you read a single verse. Psalm 119:18 is a perfect model: “Open my eyes, that I may behold wondrous things out of your law.” Diligent study is not optional for the growing Christian — it is the pathway to maturity itself (2 Timothy 2:15).

Step 1 — Read

Start by reading the Bible consistently and repeatedly. For the Old Testament, read through it at least once a year in a narrative fashion. Make notes in the margins, and write down anything you don’t immediately understand. Many questions will be answered by the text itself as you keep reading.

For the New Testament, try a different approach: read one book at a time, repetitiously, for thirty days straight. Start with a short book like 1 John and read it all the way through in one sitting every day for a month. By the end, you will know that book. For longer books like John, divide it into three sections of seven chapters and read each section daily for thirty days. In less than three years you can know the entire New Testament this way. There is no substitute for this kind of immersive, repeated reading.

As you read, write down the theme of each chapter on a small card. Memorize those themes as you go. You will be amazed at how quickly you begin to know where things are in the Bible and what each book is about.

Step 2 — Interpret

As you read, always keep one question in mind: “What does this mean?” Philip asked the Ethiopian eunuch exactly this in Acts 8:30: “Do you understand what you are reading?” It is not enough to read the text and jump straight to application. You must first determine what it means, or the application may be wrong.

The most foundational principle of interpretation is this: interpret the Bible with the Bible. This is what the Reformers called the analogy of faith — Holy Scripture is its own interpreter. The supreme standard for understanding any verse is the overall teaching of Scripture. If your interpretation of one passage contradicts the clear teaching of another, your interpretation needs adjustment, not the Bible.

A few essential rules to keep you on track:

  • Read it like any other book. God communicated through real human authors using real words, grammar, and literary forms. If it is poetry, read it as poetry. If it is history, read it as history. Do not impose a mystical meaning that bypasses the plain sense of the text.
  • Take the literal sense seriously. Interpret the text according to its natural, intended meaning in its original context. This does not mean ignoring figures of speech — it means not reading into the text something that is not there. The literal sense is the author’s intended sense.
  • Let the explicit govern the implicit. Do not build major doctrines on what is only implied. Let the clear, explicit teaching of Scripture anchor your understanding of what is harder to interpret.
  • Let the clear interpret the obscure. Every Bible reader encounters difficult passages. The rule is simple: let the clear passages illuminate the obscure ones, never the other way around. Never overturn plain teaching based on an ambiguous text.
  • Use cross-references and concordances. Let Scripture shed light on Scripture. Free tools like Blue Letter Bible give you access to original words, cross-references, and commentaries at no cost.

Step 3 — Evaluate

After reading and forming an interpretation, consult others to confirm you are on solid ground. Read Bible introductions, commentaries, and background books. The church has been studying Scripture for two thousand years. Stand on the shoulders of godly men who came before you. Reading commentaries by men like John Calvin and Matthew Henry is an excellent way to benefit from scholars whose work still serves the church centuries later.

If your interpretation puts a passage in conflict with the rest of Scripture, the interpretation needs adjustment — not the Bible. And if a passage remains unclear after your own study, bring it to your pastor or a trusted teacher. That is not weakness. That is wisdom.

Step 4 — Apply

Bible study that never changes how you live has missed the point. After reading, interpreting, and evaluating, ask the most personal question of all: “What does this mean for me?”

James 1:22 is direct: “Be doers of the word, and not hearers only, deceiving yourselves.” Sound interpretation must lead to obedient application. The goal of Bible study is not information — it is transformation.

Step 5 — Correlate

The final step is connecting what you have learned in a particular passage to the larger teaching of the whole Bible. Scripture is one book in sixty-six parts. Its truths are taught repeatedly, in a variety of ways and circumstances, from Genesis to Revelation. By cross-referencing and correlating what you study, you begin to build a sound doctrinal foundation on which to stand for the rest of your life.

Read with Humility

Running through all of this must be a spirit of humility. It is possible to be sincere in your understanding of a passage and sincerely wrong. Humility keeps us open to correction — by the text itself, by the church, by godly teachers, and by the Holy Spirit. Pride in Bible study is especially dangerous because it can dress itself up as conviction.

The great challenge of biblical interpretation is not technical — it is spiritual. The person who approaches God’s Word with a humble, teachable heart, willing to be changed by what it says, is already well ahead of the person who comes to the text looking for confirmation of what they already believe.

Practical Tips to Get Started Today

  • Get a good study Bible. The ESV Study Bible is one of the most thorough and trustworthy study Bibles available, with extensive notes, maps, articles, and cross-references to help you understand the text in context.
  • Start with 1 John. It is short, rich, and covers the essentials of the Christian life. Read it through every day for thirty days and watch what happens.
  • Keep a notebook. Write down what you observe, what you do not understand, and what you sense God applying to your life. Writing forces clarity of thought.
  • Use free online tools. Blue Letter Bible gives you access to concordances, original language tools, cross-references, and commentaries at no cost.
  • Study in community. The teaching ministry of a local church is indispensable to personal Bible study. Do not study in isolation. Bring your questions to a pastor, a small group, or a trusted fellow believer.
  • Read it every day. Joshua 1:8 commands meditating on God’s Word day and night. Consistency over time is far more powerful than occasional marathon sessions.
  • Teach what you are learning. The best way to truly know Scripture is to share it with someone else. The things you understand well enough to explain are the things that will stay with you for a lifetime.

A Closing Word

It has often been said that the Bible can be made to say anything — and sadly, in the hands of people who approach it carelessly or with an agenda, that can appear to be true. The answer is not to read less. It is to read better, more carefully, and more prayerfully, with sound principles guiding every step.

Open God’s Word expecting Him to speak. Come to it daily. Come to it humbly. Come to it hungry. He has promised that His Word will not return to Him empty (Isaiah 55:11). That promise is for you.
