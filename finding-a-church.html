How to Find a Good Church

This page draws heavily from the teaching of John MacArthur, whose multi-part sermon series Characteristics of a True Church and Marks of a Faithful Church — available at gty.org — is one of the most thorough biblical treatments of this question ever preached.

Finding a church isn't like finding a restaurant. You're not looking for the best atmosphere, the coolest music, or the most convenient parking. You're looking for a place where God is truly present — where His Word is preached, His people are being shaped, and His glory is the point.

No church is going to be perfect. Some may be in seemingly excellent condition; others are obvious fixer-uppers. What matters is that you seek God's will and evaluate not just what a church can do for you, but how you and your family can contribute to that ministry so it becomes truly a church home. Here is what Scripture says to look for.

1. The Bible Is the Absolute Authority

This is the non-negotiable. The first mark of a true church is its commitment to the absolute authority of Scripture. The pastor and elders should treat the Bible as the final word on everything — not culture, not felt needs, not tradition. If a church bends the Bible to accommodate popular opinion, keep walking.

A church that takes Scripture seriously will practice expository preaching — working through the text of God's Word verse by verse, letting God say what He says. As MacArthur puts it, expository preaching lets the Bible speak. When you unpack the text, God speaks — and that is far different from a preacher simply claiming “the Bible says.”

Source: Characteristics of a True Church, Part 1 — John MacArthur (gty.org)

2. Worship Is God-Centered, Not People-Centered

The second mark of a true church is a commitment to worship that is God-centered and Christ-centered — focused outside itself on the one who is the object of worship.

Ask yourself: does the service feel designed to impress visitors, or to glorify God? Is Jesus the point of the music, the prayers, and the sermon — or is the experience built around making people comfortable? A genuine church gathers to encounter the living God, not to put on a show.

Source: Characteristics of a True Church, Part 2 — John MacArthur (gty.org)

3. Doctrinal Clarity — They Know What They Believe

A true church is doctrinally clear. It is a collection of the people of God who know what they believe. There is nothing vague about it, nothing wavering, nothing simplistic. It is clear, profound, and marked by strong conviction.

Ask for a statement of faith. Find out what they believe about salvation, Scripture, the nature of God, and the person of Christ. Every great ministry in the history of the church was founded on sound doctrine — not on experiences, not on feelings, not on emotions, but on sound doctrine.

Source: Characteristics of a True Church, Part 2 — John MacArthur (gty.org)

4. Spiritual Discernment

A fourth element that flows out of doctrinal clarity is spiritual discernment — the ability to look at the world and understand it through the lens of Scripture. A healthy church doesn't chase every trend, platform, or personality. It tests things against God's Word and holds fast to what is true.

Look for a church that isn't afraid to say “no” to things that contradict Scripture, even when culture applauds them. As the Bereans in Acts 17:11 searched the Scriptures daily to test what they heard, a discerning church trains its people to do the same.

Source: Characteristics of a True Church, Part 1 — John MacArthur (gty.org)

5. A Pursuit of Holiness

Another mark of the true church is the pursuit of holiness — true spirituality, not legalism; Christ-likeness. A good church will call people to grow, not just to show up. You should leave convicted and encouraged, not just entertained. The congregation should look increasingly like Jesus over time.

Source: Characteristics of a True Church, Part 2 — John MacArthur (gty.org)

6. Godly, Accountable Leadership

A true church submits to a plurality of godly leaders. Healthy churches aren't built around a celebrity pastor — they're led by humble elders and pastors who serve the flock. The only authority a pastor truly possesses is the delegated authority that comes through the Word of God. When a pastor gets beyond biblical authority, he has abused his position.

Look for leaders whose lives match their preaching. You judge leaders by their fidelity to the Word of God — both in what they say and how they live.

Source: Characteristics of a True Church, Part 3 — John MacArthur (gty.org)

7. Commitment to Discipleship

A true church is committed to the full spiritual development of every believer. It's not enough to get people saved and send them on their way. A healthy church invests in people — through Bible study, mentorship, community groups, and accountability — so they grow from spiritual infancy into maturity.

MacArthur notes that there is a troubling trend of churches telling new believers to “get out” once they're saved and go find a discipleship church elsewhere. A biblical church does both — it reaches the lost and it grows the found.

Source: Characteristics of a True Church, Part 3 — John MacArthur (gty.org)

8. Mutual Love and Real Community

The church is a fellowship of people engaged in loving ministry to one another. You should find people who know each other, care for each other, and serve each other. The early church in Acts 2 was marked by shared life, generosity, prayer, and meals together. That is still the standard.

Source: Characteristics of a True Church, Part 4 — John MacArthur (gty.org)

9. Passionate Evangelism

A faithful church makes the gospel believable by the way it lives. A church that is genuinely holy, genuinely loving, and genuinely devoted to Christ will naturally draw people in. Evangelism flows from the character of the church — not just its programs.

As the old German philosopher Heine put it: “Show me your redeemed lives and I might believe in your Redeemer.” That challenge is one a healthy church meets every day.

Source: Marks of a Faithful Church — John MacArthur (gty.org)

A Note on What to Overlook

No church will check every box perfectly. Style of music, the building, the size, the dress code — these things don't define a true church. What matters is whether Christ is preached, the Bible is honored, and the people are being genuinely transformed by the Gospel.

If a church is committed to the authority of Scripture, God-centered worship, doctrinal clarity, spiritual discernment, personal holiness, godly leaders, discipleship, love, fellowship, and prayer — it will have an impact on the community, and evangelism will be the natural product.

Start there. Pray. Visit. Ask hard questions. And trust that the Lord will lead you to the people He has already prepared for you.
