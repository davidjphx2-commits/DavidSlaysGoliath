What Is the Bible?

Most people think of the Bible as a single book. It is not. It is a library — a collection of sixty-six books written by approximately forty different authors, across roughly fifteen hundred years, in three languages (Hebrew, Aramaic, and Greek), on three continents (Asia, Africa, and Europe). And yet, from beginning to end, it tells one unified story: the story of a holy God redeeming a broken world through Jesus Christ.

That kind of unity across that kind of diversity is itself remarkable. No human editorial committee planned it. The authors ranged from kings to shepherds, from fishermen to physicians, from military commanders to tax collectors. They wrote in vastly different times and places. And yet the themes, the promises, and the person they point to remain strikingly consistent from Genesis to Revelation. Christians have always understood this coherence as evidence of a single divine Author working through many human voices.

The Old Testament — 39 Books

The Old Testament is the story of God’s relationship with the people of Israel — how He created the world, how humanity fell into sin, how He called a nation to Himself, gave them His law, led them through history, and repeatedly promised a coming Redeemer. It is the foundation on which the New Testament stands. Jesus Himself said He came not to abolish the Law and the Prophets but to fulfill them (Matthew 5:17).

The thirty-nine books of the Protestant Old Testament fall into four major categories:

The Law — The Foundation (5 Books)

Also called the Pentateuch or the Torah, these five books written by Moses form the bedrock of all Scripture. Genesis opens with creation and the fall of humanity. Exodus records God delivering His people from slavery in Egypt. Leviticus, Numbers, and Deuteronomy contain the law God gave Israel — not as a means of salvation, but as the standard for how a holy God’s people were to live and worship.

History — The Story of a Nation (12 Books)

From Joshua through Esther, these books trace Israel’s history across nearly a thousand years — entering the Promised Land, the era of the judges, the rise and fall of the kings, the division of the kingdom, the Assyrian and Babylonian exiles, and the eventual return of a remnant to the land. They are not merely history for history’s sake. They show how a nation rises when it follows God and falls when it turns away.

Poetry and Wisdom — The Heart of Israel (5 Books)

Job wrestles with suffering. Psalms is the hymnbook of Israel, covering the full spectrum of human emotion before God. Proverbs gathers practical wisdom for daily life. Ecclesiastes confronts the emptiness of life without God. Song of Solomon celebrates the beauty of love. These books speak the language of the heart and have sustained believers through every kind of human experience for three thousand years.

Prophecy — The Promise of What Is Coming (17 Books)

The prophets were not primarily fortune-tellers. They were God’s spokesmen, calling Israel back to faithfulness and warning of the consequences of rebellion. But woven through their words is something extraordinary: hundreds of specific prophecies pointing to a coming Messiah. The Major Prophets — Isaiah, Jeremiah, Lamentations, Ezekiel, and Daniel — are so named for their length, not their importance. The twelve Minor Prophets from Hosea to Malachi are shorter but equally vital. Malachi, the last of the Old Testament prophets, ends with a promise: a messenger is coming to prepare the way. Then four hundred years of silence — until the New Testament opens.

The New Testament — 27 Books

The New Testament is the fulfillment of everything the Old Testament promised. It opens with the arrival of Jesus Christ — the promised Messiah — and closes with the vision of His return and the restoration of all things. It was written between approximately AD 45 and AD 95, entirely within the lifetime or memory of those who knew Jesus personally.

The Gospels — Eyewitness Accounts of Jesus (4 Books)

Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John are not biographies in the modern sense. They are testimonies — careful accounts of the life, teachings, death, and resurrection of Jesus Christ, written by or based on the witness of those who were there. Matthew was one of the twelve disciples. Mark recorded the testimony of the Apostle Peter. Luke, a physician, conducted careful interviews with eyewitnesses (Luke 1:1–4). John was the disciple closest to Jesus, writing his account last to address specific theological questions.

Each Gospel presents Jesus from a distinct angle. Matthew presents Him as the fulfillment of Old Testament prophecy — the promised Jewish Messiah. Mark presents Him as the powerful Servant of God, moving with urgency and authority. Luke presents Him as the Savior of all humanity, with special attention to the poor, the outcast, and the forgotten. John presents Him as the eternal Word of God, the divine Son who existed before creation.

Acts — The Birth of the Church (1 Book)

Written by Luke as a sequel to his Gospel, Acts records what happened after the resurrection — the outpouring of the Holy Spirit at Pentecost, the explosive growth of the early church, and the spread of the gospel from Jerusalem outward to the ends of the earth. It follows the ministry of Peter among the Jews and Paul among the Gentiles, tracing the fulfillment of Jesus’s commission in Matthew 28.

Paul’s Letters — Doctrine for the Church (13 Books)

The Apostle Paul wrote thirteen of the twenty-seven books of the New Testament. Nine are letters addressed to local churches — in Rome, Corinth, Galatia, Ephesus, Philippi, Colossae, and Thessalonica — addressing specific theological questions, correcting errors, and building believers up in the faith. Four are personal letters to individual pastors and co-workers: Timothy, Titus, and Philemon. Together they form the theological backbone of Christian doctrine, covering salvation, grace, the church, the resurrection, Christian ethics, and the person of Christ.

The General Letters — Wisdom for the Wider Church (8 Books)

Hebrews, James, 1 and 2 Peter, 1, 2 and 3 John, and Jude were written to broader audiences rather than specific congregations. Hebrews makes the case that Christ is the fulfillment of the entire Old Testament sacrificial system. James insists that genuine faith produces genuine works. Peter writes to persecuted believers about suffering and hope. John writes about love, truth, and assurance of salvation. Jude warns against false teachers who had crept into the church.

Revelation — The End of the Story (1 Book)

The final book of the Bible is a letter of hope to a persecuted church, written by the Apostle John while exiled on the island of Patmos. Using rich prophetic imagery rooted in the Old Testament, it reveals the ultimate destiny of history: Christ returns, evil is defeated, the dead are raised, and God makes all things new. It is not primarily a timeline of events to decode. It is a thunderous declaration that the Lamb who was slain has won, and that all of history is moving toward His triumph.

One Story, Beginning to End

The Bible is not a random collection of religious writings. It is a single, unified story with a beginning, a middle, and an end. It moves from creation to fall, from promise to fulfillment, from the birth of the church to the restoration of all things. Every part of it — the law, the history, the poetry, the prophecy, the Gospels, the letters — fits inside that story. And at the center of that story stands one Person: Jesus Christ. As Jesus Himself told the disciples on the road to Emmaus, beginning with Moses and all the Prophets, He explained to them what was said in all the Scriptures concerning Himself (Luke 24:27).

Can We Trust the Bible? — The Evidence for Reliability

Trusting the Bible requires more than faith in a religious tradition. There are serious, substantive reasons why historians, archaeologists, and scholars across many disciplines have concluded that the biblical text is extraordinarily well-preserved and historically reliable. The evidence falls into two main categories: the Old Testament and the New.

The Old Testament — Preserved with Extraordinary Care

The Jewish scribal tradition treated the copying of Scripture as a sacred duty of the highest order. The Masoretes — a community of Jewish scribes working from approximately AD 500 to AD 1000 — developed an elaborate system of safeguards to ensure that not a single letter was copied incorrectly. They counted the words and letters in every book. They identified the middle letter of the entire Torah. If a copy contained even one error, the entire manuscript was destroyed and the work started over. This was not mere tradition. It was reverence for the Word of God, pursued with meticulous discipline across centuries.

But the most dramatic confirmation of the Old Testament’s reliability came not from a scholar’s study — it came from a cave.

The Dead Sea Scrolls — Discovery of the Century

In the spring of 1947, a young Bedouin shepherd tossed a stone into a cave on the northwestern shore of the Dead Sea near Qumran — and heard something shatter. What he found inside changed biblical scholarship forever. Between 1947 and 1956, eleven caves in the Qumran region yielded approximately 900 manuscripts, including fragments of every book of the Old Testament except Esther. The scrolls dated from roughly 250 BC to AD 68, making them over a thousand years older than the previously oldest known Hebrew manuscripts.

Before the Dead Sea Scrolls, the oldest complete Hebrew Old Testament manuscripts dated to around AD 1000. Scholars had long assumed the text was reliable, but they had no way to check it against anything much earlier. The Dead Sea Scrolls provided exactly that check — and the results were stunning.

Old Testament scholar Gleason Archer examined the two Isaiah scrolls found in Cave 1 and reported that even though they were a thousand years earlier than the previously oldest known manuscripts, they were word-for-word identical with the standard Hebrew Bible in more than 95 percent of the text. The remaining five percent consisted almost entirely of obvious scribal slips and variations in spelling — nothing that affects any doctrine or historical claim. One of the most significant prophetic books in the entire Bible had been copied with extraordinary faithfulness across a thousand years of transmission.

The New Testament — The Most Documented Ancient Text in History

The New Testament’s manuscript evidence is without parallel in the ancient world. To appreciate what this means, consider the evidence for other major works of antiquity that historians accept as reliable without question:

The numbers are staggering. While most accepted works of antiquity survive on fewer than twenty manuscripts, the New Testament is supported by over 24,000 manuscript copies or portions — including approximately 5,800 in the original Greek, over 10,000 in Latin, and thousands more in other ancient languages. The earliest fragments date to within decades of the original writings.

Noted British manuscript scholar Sir Frederic Kenyon wrote: “The interval then between the dates of original composition and the earliest extant evidence becomes so small as to be in fact negligible, and the last foundation for any doubt that the Scriptures have come down to us substantially as they were written has now been removed.”

F.F. Bruce, one of the twentieth century’s leading New Testament scholars, put it plainly: “There is no body of ancient literature in the world which enjoys such a wealth of good attestation as the New Testament.”

Beyond the manuscript count, the New Testament is confirmed by the writings of the early church fathers — men like Clement of Rome, Ignatius, Polycarp, and Irenaeus — who quoted extensively from the New Testament within decades of its composition. Scholars have calculated that from the writings of the church fathers alone, we could reconstruct virtually the entire New Testament even if every manuscript were lost.

A Word About Canon — How the Bible Came Together

The word canon comes from a Greek word meaning “measuring rod” or “standard.” When applied to Scripture, it refers to the collection of books recognized as the authoritative Word of God. A common misconception is that church councils invented the canon — that a group of powerful men sat down and voted on which books would be “in” the Bible. The historical reality is more interesting.

The Old Testament canon was largely settled among the Jewish people well before the time of Christ. Jesus and the apostles quoted from it extensively, treating it as the authoritative Word of God. The New Testament canon developed through a process of recognition, not invention. The early church did not create the canon — it identified it. The standard they applied was essentially threefold: Was the book apostolic in origin (written by an apostle or someone in their immediate circle)? Was its teaching consistent with what Christ and the apostles had taught? Had the church universally and consistently received it as authoritative?

By the time Athanasius listed the twenty-seven books of the New Testament in his Easter letter of AD 367 — the exact same list in every Bible today — he was not announcing something new. He was formalizing what the church had already recognized and practiced for generations.
